Introduction

Learn the basics of layer height and how it affects printing. Layer height is a simple setting that impacts print speed, detail, durability, and overall appearance.

What is layer height and why it matters

Layer height is the exact thickness of each extruded or cured layer. You set it in your slicer, and it has a bigger effect on results than you might expect. Used well, it can improve speed, resolution, and smoothness.

Layer height is commonly measured in microns (1 micron = 0.001 mm). On FDM printers, smaller layers can improve XY precision for small moving features, such as print‑in‑place parts.

Different printers have different limits. Typical minimums are 50–100 microns (0.05–0.1 mm).

Now let’s see how to choose layer height for speed, resolution, and smoothness.

Optimizing print speed

One common reason to change layer height is to speed up printing. Larger layers mean fewer layers to reach the same model height, so prints finish faster (and can cost less).

Generally, increasing layer height reduces resolution and surface quality. It’s best for large prints where close‑up detail is unimportant or will be refined during post‑processing.

You can optimize layer height per model to balance speed and resolution, but effective tuning is model‑specific and may require iteration.

Boosting resolution

A lesser‑known fact: most printers have higher vertical than horizontal resolution. Lead screws provide very precise Z‑axis positioning compared to belt‑driven XY axes. This allows excellent vertical detail — one reason lithophanes print so well.

This helps explain why the Fillenium Malcon design by aaskedall is popular with Star Wars fans; it retains impressive detail on many machines. Other designers now leverage this property too.

Lower layer heights slow printing and may increase service costs, but more layers at the same height yield greater detail and smoother results.

Smoother surfaces

Another benefit of smaller layers is smoother surfaces: layer lines are less pronounced. Larger layers leave more visible lines and rougher surfaces.

Reduce layer height for parts that need smooth motion or mating, like shafts or print‑in‑place mechanisms. This increases detail but slows printing, so large parts at low heights take long and can raise the risk of print failure.

What layer height should I choose?

If you need speed, choose a larger height. For artistic parts needing fine detail, go smaller.
A good general‑purpose value is 0.2 mm. With a standard 0.4 mm nozzle, a rule of thumb is to use about 50% of the nozzle diameter for layer height.

Pros and cons of larger layer heights

  • Faster prints that may cost less when ordering
  • Great for large parts where close‑up detail is less important
  • Rougher surfaces and more visible lines than smaller heights

Pros and cons of smaller layer heights

  • Higher detail and accuracy
  • Smoother surfaces; less visible layering
  • Slower prints; very long jobs can increase failure risk
