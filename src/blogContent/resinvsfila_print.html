Introduction

Common questions compare resin and filament printing on model quality, material cost, and print time. Sometimes a part needs specific mechanical properties like tensile strength or impact resistance — how do resin prints compare to filament prints? This article covers the trade‑offs.

Durability

For strength, FDM‑printed parts are generally stronger than resin‑printed ones — for both impact resistance and tensile strength. Popular filaments like ABS, PLA, and PETG typically outperform standard resins.

Tensile strength measures the stress a material withstands when pulled until it breaks; it varies by material and part construction. Testing should consider the actual manufacturing method to be accurate.

Standard resin prints are usually less impact‑resistant, and UV exposure may continue curing over time. By contrast, PLA is inexpensive but has low heat resistance.

Test Results

PLA

The bed was set to 60 °C and hooks were printed without enclosure. The PLA hook held 129.274 kg, about 64.4 MPa tensile strength. PLA loses tensile strength over time, so it’s best for intended uses rather than high‑tensile applications.

PETG

PETG measured 50.0 MPa — surprisingly lower than PLA. In another third‑party test comparing PLA and PETG with vertical and horizontal specimens, the bending test showed vertically printed PLA handled just under 8 kg vs. PETG’s 5.9 kg (testing layer adhesion). Horizontally, PLA reached 16.6 kg while PETG didn’t fail, bending without breaking. In short, PETG is more flexible and resists breaking under some loads, but PLA has higher tensile strength.

ABS

Under ISO 527, ABS measured 38.6 MPa, weaker than PLA and PETG. In the hook test, ABS failed immediately at the same 129 kg load. While ABS is a true engineering material used daily, most applications don’t demand very high tensile strength.

Gyanta

Standard resin is often seen as weak in tensile strength, and results align: standard resin measured 23.4 MPa, well below the others. Still, SLA materials have advanced rapidly, with tensile strength a focus area — manufacturers are working hard to meet growing industry demands.

Resin vs Filament Impact Strength

Impact strength (measured in J/m) differs from tensile strength; it measures how much impact energy a standard specimen absorbs before failure. The IZOD test was used, with flat, vertical, and horizontal orientations.

As shown, results vary by material and orientation. In the vertical test, PLA scored highest at 77.42 J/m, PETG lowest at 35.45 J/m.

Resin impact strength was assessed with the IZOD test. As an isotropic material, only one specimen orientation was required for resin.

How to Strengthen an FDM Part

Depending on the application, strength matters. Methods to increase FDM part strength include using straight‑line infill (density above 50%), increasing extrusion width, increasing perimeters (wall thickness), and lowering layer height.

Summary

For strong, mechanically resistant prints, filament is usually the best option.
For high‑detail, aesthetic models, resin is recommended.
Use the comparisons above to decide whether a material’s strength and durability match your goals.
