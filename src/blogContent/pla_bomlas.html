Introduction

PLA, the most popular 3D printing material, is often said to be biodegradable. Polylactic Acid (PLA) is a thermoplastic and the most widely used FDM filament. It’s easy to print, extrudes around 180 °C, and usually needs no heated bed.

PLA is also considered a bioplastic made from renewable sources and potentially biodegradable. However, claims about PLA biodegradability are debated and depend on conditions.

Composition

PLA is considered a bioplastic made from renewable biomass. It consists of lactic acid units — small organic acids also found in familiar foods like sourdough bread, yogurt, soy sauce, and corn. In principle, anything containing glucose can be converted to lactic acid molecules.

These lactic acid units form the basis of PLA and give it its general mechanical and printing properties. PLA’s relatively low crystallinity leads to a low glass‑transition temperature (Tg).

The low Tg makes PLA easy to melt and shape, which is why it prints easily. The tradeoff is reduced heat resistance compared to some other plastics.

Other common materials like PETG and ABS (petroleum‑based) are typically stronger but harder to print. Their polymers usually have higher crystallinity and Tg, improving strength and temperature resistance at the cost of printability.

Additives

Many PLA filaments contain additional chemicals and materials referred to as additives. They dilute PLA “purity,” but make it more practical for printing or other uses.

Manufacturers use a wide range of additives with different compositions and purposes alongside the base PLA. Blends address material properties, print performance, and color (PLA is naturally a milky clear).

Most PLA+ filaments claim higher toughness than standard PLA by using impact‑modifier additives designed to reduce brittleness and improve impact resistance.

Extraction and production

The base inputs are lactic acids sourced from organisms and plants that contain glucose. Corn is a common feedstock because it contains sufficient glucose.

To extract lactic acid from corn, the plant undergoes a wet‑milling process to separate starch. The starch is then combined with other reagents and heated to isolate sugars. The glucose is fermented to obtain lactic‑acid monomers.

These lactic acids are then turned into PLA through either polymerization (most common) or condensation, using catalysts and heat to link the monomers into PLA polymer chains.

Implications

In theory, PLA is a renewable bioplastic because it originates from plant‑derived lactic acids. However, plants are only the starting point. Additives and intensive processing are also involved. While the PLA fraction of a product may be biodegradable, additives like impact modifiers often are not.

Degradation time correlates with crystallinity: lower crystallinity is generally easier for microorganisms to break down. PLA typically has relatively low crystallinity compared to many petroleum‑based plastics, but adding high‑crystallinity components can reduce biodegradability and slow breakdown.

Manufacturing steps such as fermentation, polymerization/condensation, and the use of catalysts can also affect the biodegradability of certain components. Polymerization tends to increase crystallinity, which slows degradation. In short, formulation and processing matter.

Biodegradation

Contrary to common belief, PLA can take hundreds of years to break down in typical environments like landfills or backyard compost. Leaving PLA parts outdoors for years usually does little to the material’s composition — PLA is still a plastic, and plastics do not readily degrade in nature.

Degradation can be accelerated in specialized, industrial composting facilities that maintain the right conditions.

Many PLA formulations exist; some approach near‑100% biodegradability under proper conditions

Strict environmental requirements

Effective PLA biodegradation requires controlled conditions typically found in industrial facilities. Two main requirements are:

  • Microbes: Sufficient populations of microorganisms that can digest PLA and break it down at a chemical level. Far higher counts are needed than in ordinary soil.
  • Heat: PLA’s glass‑transition temperature is about 60 °C. Near this temperature, PLA softens and can deform, increasing surface area and enabling microbes to act more effectively.

Note that melting PLA does not directly cause biodegradation; heat primarily enables biological and chemical processes to proceed. Microbial activity generally increases above ~30 °C. Moisture also matters: higher humidity accelerates hydrolysis and speeds chemical breakdown. So, is PLA biodegradable? Practically speaking, not in everyday conditions. Unless PLA parts are sent to industrial composting facilities, they will likely persist for decades or centuries along with ABS and PETG.

Conclusion

While PLA is technically biodegradable, in practice it seldom biodegrades outside controlled settings. Perspective matters: compared to fruit like strawberries, PLA is not biodegradable at all on human timescales; compared to many petroleum plastics (e.g., ABS, film bags), PLA’s potential biodegradability can be considered relatively better — but still conditional.

Other plastics

Most other common plastics, including ABS, are petroleum‑based. They can take centuries to degrade in natural environments. Because they are not bio‑based, microbes struggle to digest them, and even when breakdown occurs, petroleum‑based residues can still harm ecosystems. In this respect, PLA can be a relatively more eco‑friendly filament choice — with the caveat that composting conditions matter.
