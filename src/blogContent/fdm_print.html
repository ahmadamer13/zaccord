Introduction

Learn the basics of Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) 3D printing. See why it’s affordable and great for fast, low‑cost prototyping. Explore FDM materials along with pros and cons.

Fused Deposition Modeling (also known as FFF) is an additive process in the material extrusion category. The printer selectively deposits molten thermoplastic along a toolpath, layer by layer. Materials are thermoplastics supplied as filament.

FDM printer building a model layer by layer

FDM is the most widely used 3D printing technology. It has the largest installed base worldwide and is often the first 3D printing tech people encounter. This article explains FDM’s basics and key traits to help you get great results.

How does FDM work?

A spool of thermoplastic filament is loaded. Once the nozzle reaches target temperature, the filament is driven into the hotend and melts.

The printhead moves on a 3‑axis system (X/Y/Z). Molten material is extruded as thin strands and deposited at precise locations each layer, where it cools and solidifies. Fans can accelerate cooling.

Multiple passes fill areas, similar to coloring a rectangle with a marker. After a layer completes, either the bed moves down or the head moves up, and the next layer is printed. This repeats until the part is complete.

FDM printer parameters

Most systems let you set nozzle and bed temperatures, print speed, layer height, and fan speed.

Build volume and layer height are key. Desktop printers often range from 200×200×200 mm up to 500×500×500 mm. For small beds, large parts can be split and reassembled.

Typical layer heights are 100–400 microns. Smaller layers improve surface finish and curved geometry accuracy, while larger layers print faster and can reduce cost.

Characteristics of FDM prints

Bár az FDM technológiával működő 3D nyomtatók extrudáló rendszerei között vannak eltérések, és az alkatrészek különböző minőségűek lehetnek, van néhány közös jellemző (néhány jó, néhány rossz), amelyre FDM-nyomtatáskor számíthatsz.

Warping

Warping is one of the most common issues. As material cools, it shrinks. Since different regions cool at different rates, internal stresses pull layers up. Control temperatures and improve bed adhesion to reduce it.

Design choices also help:

  • Large flat areas tend to warp; avoid when possible.
  • Thin protrusions also warp; add material to increase bed contact.
  • Sharp corners warp more than filleted ones; add radii.
  • Materials vary: ABS warps more than PLA or PETG.

Layer adhesion

Strong bonding between layers is critical. Hot extruded filament presses into the previous layer; heat and pressure fuse them.

Interlayer strength is always less than bulk strength, making FDM parts anisotropic: weaker in Z than in XY. Consider part orientation in design.

Example: With ABS at 50% infill, horizontal (XY) tensile specimens can be nearly 4× stronger than vertical ones, and elongate almost 10× more before breaking.

Supports

Molten filament cannot be deposited in mid‑air, so some geometries need supports. Removing supports can be difficult, so design to minimize them.

Supports are usually the same material as the part. Soluble supports exist but are mostly for high‑end printers; they can increase cost without always improving quality.

Infill and wall thickness

To save time and material, FDM parts are rarely printed solid. Printers lay down multiple outer walls and use a lower‑density internal infill.

Infill percentage and wall thickness greatly affect strength. A common default is 20% infill and 1.2 mm walls, a practical compromise between strength and speed.

A major FDM strength is material variety: from general thermoplastics (PLA, ABS) to engineering grades (TPU, PETG) and high‑performance plastics (PEI).

Material choice impacts strength, accuracy, and cost.

Summary

Advantages of FDM

  • FDM is the most cost‑effective way to produce custom thermoplastic parts and prototypes.
  • Az átfutási idők rövidek (jellemzően néhány nap) a technológia elterjedtsége miatt.
  • Anyagok széles választéka áll rendelkezésre, amelyek mind prototípusok készítésére, mind egyes funkcionális alkalmazásokhoz alkalmasak.
  • Az FDM segítségével gyorsan és költséghatékonyan készíthetők prototípusok és funkcionális alkatrészek.
  • Az FDM-hez az anyagok széles skálája áll rendelkezésre.

Milyen hátrányai vannak az FDM 3D nyomtatásnak?

  • Az FDM-nek van a legalacsonyabb felbontása a többi 3D nyomtatási technológiához képest, ezért nem alkalmas nagyon apró részleteket tartalmazó alkatrészekhez.
  • Az alkatrészek valószínűleg látható rétegvonalakkal rendelkeznek, ezért a sima felület érdekében utófeldolgozásra van szükség.
  • A rétegtapadási mechanizmus miatt az alkatrészek eredendően anizotrópok.
  • Az FDM eleve anizotróp, ezért mechanikailag kritikus alkatrészekhez nem alkalmas.
