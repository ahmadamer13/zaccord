Introduction

3D printing offers huge creative potential, but buying a printer isn’t right for everyone. Many people buy a machine and filament expecting to print anything right away — until something breaks and they don’t know how to proceed. Some discover they don’t enjoy the process after purchasing.
Here’s a realistic framework to decide if you should buy a 3D printer before investing significant money.

Industrial FDM printer line in operation

What 3D Printers Cannot Do

Many myths and misinformation surround 3D printers. Maker communities know more, but beginners often don’t — avoid being misled by hype around the technology.

1. You Can Print Anything You Imagine

Technically it can be true with the right technology, but if those technologies aren’t accessible to you, or you lack 3D modeling experience, it may not be feasible.
Early on, if you don’t know how to create your own designs, you’ll likely download others’ models that were optimized for printing.

2. The Technology Always Saves Money

Savings can happen long‑term, but often aren’t large. For many needs, buying items is cheaper than spending hours modeling, printing, and iterating — sometimes still not working. Upfront costs, maintenance, parts, accessories, and your time often exceed savings. If saving money is the only goal, it may become a chore instead of a fun hobby.

3. You Can Print Many Materials

Most consumer printers handle plastics, which suffice for many projects. Printing metals, clays, or exotic materials requires specialized, expensive systems. Stick to materials with manageable melt/cure behavior; fortunately there’s a wide range of plastics with varied properties.

4. Downloading Nice Models from Thingiverse

Downloading ready‑made models (e.g., Thingiverse) is convenient but can quickly get boring. The real value is creating your own designs and only downloading when needed. Printing a dozen things you don’t need leads to burnout — and clutter.

Do Your Hobbies Align With 3D Printing?

Why do you want to get into 3D printing? Spend a moment reflecting. Do you feel you should do it “just because,” or do you have specific projects in mind?
Do you like action figures, board games, or scale models? Do you enjoy puzzles, model building, or interesting mechanisms and linkages?

If you can point to reasons like these for getting a printer, you’re likely a maker at heart.
People who also tinker in areas like electronics, laser cutting, programming, or engineering tend to enjoy 3D printing the most. The printer becomes another tool you use — not the center of every project. It helps you quickly test and improve parts.
If you only want occasional one‑off items from websites, an on‑demand printing service may be the better, more economical option.

A Few Things 3D Printers Are Great For

  • Board games: print new or replacement pieces, or design your own games.
  • Models: model almost anything to explore creativity and constraints.
  • Action figures: perfect for gamers and fantasy fans.

If your interests align with owning a printer, great — but there are still a few things to consider.

Technical Challenges of 3D Printing

Like most hobbies, 3D printing isn’t effortless. It takes time, commitment, and sometimes frustration before it pays off.

Learning 3D Modeling Software

3D modeling and CAD tools are central to 3D printing. Are you willing to learn them? Many people dislike adopting new tools, and you’ll need to pick up quite a bit before printing your first custom part.
To get the most out of this hobby, you’ll eventually want to model your own designs. Everything you print starts with a 3D model. Software like SolidWorks, Inventor, Blender, and others require time and effort.

Mastering the Printing Process

After you have a model, you must export the right file format and use a slicer to generate the printer code. It may sound complex, but it’s learnable and rewarding.
Some people get frustrated and give up. Bottom line: if you aren’t interested in design or learning, a printing service may suit you better than owning a machine.

Troubleshooting

A lot can go wrong in 3D printing. At some point you’ll likely have misconfigured settings or a jammed nozzle, so sooner or later you’ll need to troubleshoot.
Some makers embrace this as part of the hobby, focusing first on upgrading their machines to prevent issues and improve print quality.
For example, the Anet A8 is a rather weak and unreliable 3D printer, yet very popular because it’s cheap — and many owners enjoy upgrading it into a better machine.

Costs of 3D printing

People often forget the recurring costs of 3D printing — you need to buy materials regularly.
Many materials are available for different purposes. For now, here are a few of the most common:

  • ABS
  • PLA
  • PETG
  • TPU

These filaments can range widely in price and rise further for higher quality or specialty types. Keep in mind that the cheapest option rarely yields good results. On average, expect to spend a moderate amount per spool, but this adds up quickly depending on how much you print.

How much does a 3D printer cost?

This is usually where most people hesitate. 3D printers can be expensive, and looks aren’t everything.
Prices vary widely from entry‑level to high‑end home printers.
If you’re a beginner, avoid the very cheapest options as they are often unreliable and produce poor quality results.

Buy a printer or use a service?

If you work in product development, investing in your own printer can make sense.
But if you’ll only use it a few times, you’re better off sending your model to a 3D printing service.
Service costs vary by provider.
Roughly: if you need fewer than about 20 parts, sending to a service is often cheaper; above that or if you need to experiment, owning a printer may be more economical.

Conclusion

If your hobby or profession involves 3D printing and you can handle the costs and technical challenges, buying a printer may be worth it. If you’re unsure and want to try first, use a service provider. If it meets your expectations, then consider purchasing a printer.
